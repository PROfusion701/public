REFERENCE SHEET — WHO'S LYING DATASET DESIGN

This file explains how to interpret and generate question pairs for the JSON dataset.

It is NOT an output format. It is only guidance.

---

## 1. CORE IDEA

Each entry is a pair of questions:

["Question A", "Question B"]

They are designed to feel comparable but create different reasoning paths.

Players must not easily detect which question is “correct” or “opposite”.

---

## 2. HOW TO READ A PAIR

A pair is NOT:
- a direct opposite
- a paraphrase
- a logical inversion

A pair IS:
- two different perspectives on similar human behavior
- two different ways of measuring similar intent or experience
- two prompts that can produce diverging answers under pressure

---

## 3. TYPES OF PAIRS

### A. Closely related (same domain, different angle)

Meaning:
Same topic, different internal reasoning.

Example:
["What is your maximum weight you can lift?", "What is the lightest object you struggle to carry?"]

Why it works:
Both involve physical strength, but one is peak performance, the other is difficulty threshold.

---

### B. Loosely related (different context, similar scale)

Meaning:
Different situations, but comparable magnitude or intensity.

Example:
["How long can you focus without distraction?", "How long can you wait in a queue before getting impatient?"]

Why it works:
Both measure patience/endurance but in different environments.

---

### C. Unrelated but comparable (weak semantic link)

Meaning:
Different domains, but still similar “scale type” (time, effort, frequency, intensity).

Example:
["How many hours do you sleep on weekends?", "How many books do you read per year?"]

Why it works:
Both are measurable personal habits, but unrelated behaviors.

---

## 4. DESIGN RULES

- Avoid obvious symmetry (do NOT mirror words directly)
- Avoid making pairs that look like “good vs bad”
- Avoid explicit unit matching (do not reveal pairing logic)
- Avoid repetitive phrasing templates
- Prefer natural human questions over structured survey language
- Keep both questions believable independently

---

## 5. WHAT MAKES A GOOD PAIR

A good pair:
- feels like two normal questions someone might ask separately
- produces answers that can be compared socially
- creates uncertainty during discussion
- does NOT obviously reveal which is “linked” to the other

---

## 6. WHAT TO AVOID

Bad pair patterns:
- exact opposites ("high speed" vs "low speed")
- identical structure swaps ("How much X" vs "How little X")
- obvious scaling pairs (1 hour vs 10 hours)
- mirrored phrasing

---

## 7. LANGUAGE HANDLING

- EN dataset: natural conversational English
- FR dataset: natural conversational French (not literal translation of English structure)
- Do not translate word-for-word; adapt culturally

---

## 8. FINAL GOAL

The dataset should feel like:
- real human psychology questions
- not a structured survey
- not a test
- not a quiz

It should create doubt, discussion, and social inference during gameplay.
